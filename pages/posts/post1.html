Landing your first developer job without a CS degree

The degree is a proxy — what employers actually want is proof you can build things, solve problems, and keep learning. Here is the portfolio strategy, networking approach, and interview framing that gets self-taught developers hired.

DK
Dana Kim
Career Coach · Former Hiring Manager
PublishedApr 29, 2024
Read time7 min
LevelBeginner

I have interviewed hundreds of developers over twelve years. I have hired people with Computer Science PhDs and people who learned to code six months ago from YouTube. The degree has never been the deciding factor. The portfolio always has been.

This is not feel-good advice. It is a practical observation from the hiring side of the table: the question every interviewer is asking is "can this person ship things that work?" Your job is to answer that question before they ask it.

⚠️
The honest caveat Some companies — mostly large enterprises and FAANG — do still screen for degrees at the resume stage. This guide is aimed at the much larger pool of startups, agencies, and mid-size tech companies that explicitly do not.

The portfolio strategy that actually works

Most self-taught developers make the same mistake: they build too many small projects and not enough real ones. A portfolio of ten to-do apps signals "I followed tutorials." A portfolio of three projects that solve actual problems signals "I can think."

The right number of projects in your portfolio is three. Each one should demonstrate a different skill dimension:

  1. Project 1 — Depth: Something technically challenging. A real API integration, real data, real edge cases handled gracefully.
  2. Project 2 — Complexity: Multi-page, multi-feature. Proves you can architect something larger than a single component.
  3. Project 3 — Relevance: Built for the type of role you want. Applying for front-end roles? A polished, responsive UI. Applying for full-stack? Your own API and database.

Your README is half the project

Interviewers open GitHub repos constantly. Most see a wall of code with a one-line README that says "a project built with React." That is a missed opportunity.

Your README should answer four questions in under 30 seconds:

  • What does this project do and who is it for?
  • Why did you build it this way? (one paragraph on your main technical decision)
  • Where is the live demo? (non-negotiable — always deploy)
  • How do I run it locally? (three commands max)
"The developers who stood out in our hiring process were the ones who could explain their decisions, not just their code. The README was often where we first saw that ability." — Engineering Lead at a 200-person SaaS company

Framing your self-taught background

The phrase "I don't have a degree but..." puts you on the defensive before you've said anything meaningful. Reframe it completely.

✅
Instead of apologising for the gap, lead with the proof "I've spent the last 18 months building real projects and studying the same fundamentals you'd get in a CS programme — here are three things I shipped." Then shut up and let the work speak.

What to actually study

You don't need to replicate a 4-year degree. You need enough fundamentals to have intelligent conversations and solve real problems. Focus on these in order:

  • 1 Data structures and algorithms basics Arrays, objects, loops, recursion, and Big O notation at a conceptual level. You will be asked about these in interviews.
  • 2 How the web works HTTP requests and responses, the browser rendering pipeline, DNS, and what happens between you typing a URL and the page appearing.
  • 3 Git and version control Not just git commit and git push. Branching, merging, rebasing, and pull request etiquette. This is table stakes.
  • 4 The browser dev tools Network tab, console, Elements panel, Lighthouse. Knowing your tools makes you look immediately employable.

Networking that doesn't feel like networking

The word "networking" conjures images of awkward conferences and LinkedIn messages that go nowhere. The version that actually works is much simpler: be publicly useful.

Write one short article per month about something you built or learned. It doesn't need to be profound. "Here is how I solved X problem while building Y project" is enough. Post it on dev.to. Share it once. Over six months, this builds a body of work that is far more compelling than a LinkedIn profile with a list of skills.

The other thing that works: answer questions on GitHub issues for tools you use. Even closing an issue with a good reproduction step or a workaround makes you a known contributor. Maintainers notice.

What to say in the interview

For every project in your portfolio, prepare a two-minute story that covers: what you built, one decision you made and why, and one problem you hit and how you solved it. That structure maps directly to the questions you'll be asked.

When you get a technical question you don't know the answer to — and you will — say this: "I haven't run into that specific scenario, but here is how I'd approach finding the answer." Then walk through your reasoning. Interviewers hire people who know how to learn, not people who know everything.
